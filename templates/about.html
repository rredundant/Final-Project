About Our Project

For our data prediction model, we extracted text chat support transcripts from WordPress.com customers on the Personal, Premium, and Business Plans. Using classification models against vectorized text data, we predicted which plan the transcript was associated with. Because the three plans had approximately equal subscribers, we had about 33% chance to predict correctly by guessing any one plan for every chat; our models brought us closer to 50% accuracy. There was some variance amongst the 12 algorithms tried within the scikit-learn library.

To further diversify our models and hopefully achieve greater accuracy, we included Facebook’s open source library fastText. While relatively straightforward to use, fastText proved to be limited in its simplicity. Due to those limitations, we had to manually generate data for confusion matrices and related measurements. “Manually” included writing nine nested if statements to create the needed nine variables.

To compare the accuracy of our models, we took our full data set, shuffled it for every run, and ran it at each size. With fastText, the accuracy of the prediction was a direct correlation to the size of the sample. This correlation most likely denotes overfitting the data rather than an actual informed prediction. The multiple algorithms run through sci-kit learn had different peaks for optimal prediction and may therefore have more reliable accuracy. However, we feel there may just not be a reasonable or reliable way to predict the customer’s plan given the nature of this data. Perhaps customer interactions are truly universal across the three plans.

We sampled 80,000 transcripts over 16 months. Future analysis on this dataset could include sentiment analysis or analysis of how support interactions have changed over time all the way from length of interaction (time, word count) and topics covered.

Also, these models seemingly benefit from a binary set of labels, or rather a binary set of possibilities. When talking about true positives and false negatives, the buckets get messy with three labels. Jen mentioned the “Business” plan yielded the most revenue. So, another route to try for a future approach would be to consider Business a positive and the occurrence of any other plan a negative.
